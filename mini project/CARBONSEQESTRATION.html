Carbon sequestration is the process of storing carbon in a carbon pool.  It plays a crucial role in limiting climate change by reducing the amount of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. There are two main types of carbon sequestration: biologic (also called biosequestration) and geologic

Types of Carbon Sequestration

  1. Biological Sequestration
    • Forests: Trees and plants absorb CO₂ during photosynthesis and store it as biomass (trunks, branches, roots, leaves).
    • Soils: Plants transfer carbon to soils through roots and decomposing organic matter.
    • Oceans: Marine organisms, such as phytoplankton, absorb CO₂ during photosynthesis. Additionally, CO₂ can dissolve directly in seawater.
    • Wetlands: These ecosystems store carbon in plant matter and sediments.
  2. Geological Sequestration
    • This involves injecting CO₂ into underground rock formations, such as depleted oil and gas fields or deep saline aquifers, where it is trapped and stored long-term
    • Techniques include enhanced oil recovery (EOR), where CO₂ is used to extract more oil from existing fields while being stored underground.
  3. Chemical Sequestration
    • Mineral Carbonation: This process involves reacting CO₂ with naturally occurring minerals to form stable carbonates.
    • Industrial Processes: Capture and storage of CO₂ from industrial sources, such as power plants and cement factories, using technologies like amine scrubbing.

Methods and Technologies

  1. Direct Air Capture (DAC) DAC technologies extract CO₂ directly from ambient air and store it underground or use it in industrial applications.
  2. Bioenergy with Carbon Capture and Storage (BECCS) Combines biomass energy production with CO₂ capture and storage. Biomass absorbs CO₂ during growth, which is then captured and stored when the biomass is used for energy.
  3. Agricultural Practices Techniques such as no-till farming, cover cropping, and agroforestry enhance soil carbon storage.
